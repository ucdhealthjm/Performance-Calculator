What this calculator implements (as published). The deterministic mean of the Monte Carlo dose-response model in Moen 2025, Cureus 17(10): e95729, evaluated at the exact sleep-hour the user enters. Uncertainty ranges reflect within-individual variability only (z noise + parameter SD). With the post-publication toggle off (the default), outputs reproduce the paper's Table 1 to within ±1.5 units across all eight metrics. Not for clinical decision-making.

Post-publication addenda (not part of the original paper). (1) External concordance check: the per-extended-shift effect predicted by the published model (2.05× medical-error ratio at 2.5 h vs. 7 h of sleep) closely matches the directly observed OR of 1.84 in Barger et al., BMJ Med 2023 (n = 4,826 PGY2+ residents); the schedule comparison in Barger et al., Sleep 2019 (ROSTERS trial) is directionally concordant. Field-study weekly-hours effects exceed this model's acute-sleep predictions by ~3×, consistent with workload, recovery, and chronic-debt contributions the model does not include — so outputs should be read as a lower bound on real-world impairment. (2) Optional chronic-debt toggle (off by default): inflates error and lapse slopes by ~1.5× per Belenky et al., Sleep 2003. When on, the "extended model" badge appears next to the results header.

Post-publication addendum · not part of the published paper

Shift Profile — what does the marginal hour buy?

This panel models predicted physician performance across the hours of a single shift, then compares those predictions to what large randomized trials of duty-hour flexibility actually observed when shifts were extended past 16 hours. Role, specialty, and chronic-debt selections from the calculator above are inherited.

Shift parameters

The trajectory model assumes the shift starts after ~2 hours of pre-shift wake time. Within-shift impairment grows once total wake time exceeds ~16 hours, anchored to Williamson 2000 and Arnedt 2005. The per-hour sleep-debt accumulation rate is a post-publication modeling assumption, not derived in the source paper.

What randomized trials observed when shifts were allowed to extend past 16 hours

Patient outcomes — noninferior in every measure

  • 30-day mortality: 12.5% vs 12.2% (flexible vs standard). iCOMPARE patient safety [3]
  • 7-day readmission/death: 16.9% vs 16.6%. iCOMPARE [3]
  • 30-day readmission/death: 29.9% vs 29.3%. iCOMPARE [3]
  • 30-day death or serious surgical complication: 9.1% vs 9.0% (n = 138,691). FIRST trial [1]

Allowing extended shifts produced no measurable patient benefit.

Education and training — no improvement

  • In-training exam score: 68.9% vs 69.4% (flexible vs standard). iCOMPARE education [2]
  • ABSITE scores were unchanged across the 2011 ACGME shift restriction. Rajaram 2014 [5]
  • Direct patient-care time: 13.0% vs 11.8% (NS). iCOMPARE [2]
  • Education time: 7.3% vs 7.3% — identical. iCOMPARE [2]
  • Dissatisfaction with education quality: 11.0% vs 10.7%. FIRST trial [1]

No objective knowledge measure, education-time measure, or training-satisfaction measure improved with longer shifts.

Trainee well-being — substantially worse

  • Dissatisfied with overall well-being: 30% vs 15% (2.5×). iCOMPARE [2]
  • Dissatisfied with personal-life impact: 33% vs 7% (6.1×). iCOMPARE [2]
  • Dissatisfied with morale: 24% vs 4% (8.1×). iCOMPARE [2]
  • Reported fatigue affecting patient safety: 12% vs 7% (2.0×). iCOMPARE [2]
  • Reported fatigue affecting personal safety: 16% vs 8% (2.0×). iCOMPARE [2]
  • FIRST trial: surgical residents reported worse domains for time with family (3.66×), rest (3.85×), health (3.22×). [1]

Every well-being and self-reported safety measure was worse under extended shifts.

Alertness on overnight extended shifts — measurable deficit

  • On the second day of an extended overnight shift, interns averaged 5.12 h sleep per 24 h vs 7.35 h on standard night-shift days. iCOMPARE sleep substudy [4]
  • Karolinska Sleepiness Score on the post-extended-shift morning averaged 6.8 / 9 ("sleepy, but no effort to stay awake"). [4]
  • 87.7% of post-extended-shift mornings included reported excessive sleepiness; 38.6% reached "sleepy, fighting sleep" or "extremely sleepy." [4]
  • Objectively measured alertness (PVT lapses) did not meet noninferiority criteria. [4]

Even with strategic pre- and post-shift sleep, the post-shift alertness deficit is measurable and consistent.

The hypothesis that more hours produces more competent physicians has been tested. Across iCOMPARE, FIRST, and the 2011 ACGME natural experiment, neither patient outcomes nor knowledge tests nor education time improved when shifts were extended past 16 hours. Self-reported well-being and post-shift alertness consistently worsened. The shift-profile model's prediction — that the marginal hour past 16 produces continued performance decay without compensating benefit — is consistent with what these trials actually observed.
What these trials did not test. Every cited duty-hour trial compared one chronically-deprived schedule (≤ 16 h shifts, 80 h/wk, ~6–7 h average sleep) to another (≥ 24 h shifts, 80 h/wk, ~6–7 h average sleep). None tested a recovery-centric comparator with protected ≥ 7 h nightly sleep. The published paper's central argument — that the duty-hour debate has been framed entirely between two forms of chronic deprivation — is reinforced, not undermined, by these results.
